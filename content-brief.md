## Tone & Style Guide (all posts)

**Voice:** First-person, conversational, opinionated. You're talking to a smart friend over a beer, not writing a LinkedIn thought leadership post. Think Dwarkesh-style: intellectually curious, willing to say unfashionable things, grounded in real experience rather than theory.

**What to avoid:** Jargon soup, "digital transformation" buzzwords, hedging language ("it could be argued that…"), generic advice that could come from anyone. No bullet-point listicles. No stock-photo energy.

**What to lean into:** Specific stories from the shop floor. Contrarian takes backed by lived experience. Moments where you were wrong. Numbers where you have them. The gap between what consultants say and what actually happens at 3am on a production line.

**Structure:** Each essay should feel like one clear argument, not a survey. Open with a hook (a scene, a provocative claim, a question). Build the argument through 2–3 concrete examples. Close with a take that the reader will remember.

**Length:** 600–800 words. Short enough to read in one sitting, long enough to say something real.

## Blog 1: The Case for Code on the Factory Floor

**Working title:** *Why I Write Python and Run Factories (And Why More Ops Leaders Should)*

**Core argument:** The biggest leverage point in modern manufacturing isn't a new machine or a new methodology — it's an operations leader who can write a script. The gap between "the data exists" and "someone made a decision with it" is almost always a human bottleneck, and basic coding skills collapse that gap.

**Angle:** This isn't about becoming a software engineer. It's about the 80/20 of technical literacy for people who run physical operations. You're not arguing everyone should learn to code — you're arguing that the *specific combination* of operational judgement and scripting ability is absurdly undervalued.

**Key beats to hit:**

- Open with a concrete moment: a time you wrote a quick script that solved a problem that would have taken a team days to fix manually. Make it vivid — what was broken, what did you write, what happened next.
- The "translation tax" problem: every time an ops leader has to brief a data analyst or IT team to get a simple answer, that's a delay and a distortion. Most of what ops leaders need from data is simple — filtering, aggregation, visualisation. Python/SQL handles 90% of it.
- Why the manufacturing world is culturally resistant to this. The identity gap: "I'm a factory person, not a tech person." The org design gap: IT and operations as separate kingdoms.
- What "enough code" actually looks like in practice. You're not building production software. You're writing scripts that talk to APIs, parse CSVs, automate repetitive decisions. Give a specific example of what your toolkit looks like day-to-day.
- Close with the career arbitrage angle: people who can do both are rare, which means they're disproportionately valuable.

**Tone check:** Pragmatic, not evangelical. You're sharing what works for you, not prescribing a curriculum.

## Blog 2: Why AI Actually Matters on the Shop Floor

**Working title:** *AI on the Shop Floor: Ignore the Hype, Here's What's Real*

**Core argument:** Most of the AI conversation in manufacturing is either vendor-driven fantasy ("autonomous factories by 2027!") or dismissive scepticism ("my operators will never trust a model"). The truth is narrower and more interesting: AI matters in manufacturing for a handful of very specific, unsexy problems — and the bottleneck to adoption is almost never the technology.

**Angle:** You've seen both sides — the BCG-style pitch deck version and the reality of deploying systems into noisy, messy, variable production environments. Write from that dual perspective. The Dwarkesh energy here is: be willing to say what's overhyped and what's underrated, with specifics.

**Key beats to hit:**

- Open by naming a specific AI use case you've seen pitched vs. what actually happened when it hit the shop floor. The gap is the story.
- The 3 places AI genuinely changes things in manufacturing right now: (a) vision/inspection — replacing human visual checks at speed and consistency humans can't match, (b) predictive maintenance — not the magical version, but the practical "flag this before it fails" version, (c) decision support — surfacing patterns in production data that no one had time to find manually. Be specific about which of these you've seen work and which are still vapourware.
- Why the real bottleneck is data infrastructure, not algorithms. Most factories don't have clean, connected, labelled data. The AI conversation should start with "can we actually capture this signal reliably?" not "which model should we use?"
- The people problem: operators and shift leads need to trust and understand what the system is doing. If it's a black box, it gets ignored or overridden. The best AI deployments you've seen are the ones where the operator feels *more* in control, not less.
- Close with a forward-looking take: where is AI in manufacturing in 5 years, honestly? Not the conference keynote version — your actual prediction.

**Tone check:** Sceptical but not cynical. You believe AI matters — you just think 80% of the current conversation is aimed at the wrong problems.

## Blog 3: What You Can Only Learn from Running a 24/7 Factory

**Working title:** *Lessons from the Night Shift: What Running a 24/7 Factory Actually Teaches You*

**Core argument:** There are things you learn from running continuous operations that you cannot learn any other way — not from an MBA, not from consulting, not from reading about it. The 24/7 environment compresses feedback loops, exposes character, and forces a kind of operational thinking that transfers to everything else you do.

**Angle:** This is the most personal of the four essays. You ran a 24/7 R&D factory at Rolls-Royce in your mid-20s with full P&L and a team of ~50. Write from that experience. The Dwarkesh energy here is intellectual honesty about what was hard, what surprised you, and what changed how you think.

**Key beats to hit:**

- Open with a scene. A phone call at 2am. A shift handover that went wrong. Something that puts the reader on the factory floor and makes them feel the weight of continuous operations.
- Lesson 1: You learn what you actually believe about people. When things go wrong at 3am, your management philosophy gets stress-tested in real time. The gap between what leaders say they believe and what they actually do under pressure is enormous. Give a specific example.
- Lesson 2: Systems thinking becomes instinct, not theory. In a 24/7 environment, everything is connected and the consequences of a bad decision show up fast. You develop an intuition for second-order effects that you can't get from a case study.
- Lesson 3: The handover is everything. Continuous operations live or die on the quality of shift handovers. This is a microcosm of a much bigger idea: most organisational failures are transition failures, not execution failures.
- Lesson 4: You learn to make decisions with incomplete information, quickly, and live with the consequences. The 24/7 environment doesn't pause for analysis paralysis.
- Close with how these lessons show up in what you do now — running a robotics startup is a different context, but the operating instincts are the same.

**Tone check:** Reflective but not nostalgic. You're drawing transferable lessons, not telling war stories for their own sake.

## Blog 4: What Stays the Same — Scaling Manufacturing at Any Size

**Working title:** *£10bn or £10m: What Never Changes When You're Scaling Manufacturing*

**Core argument:** Having done this inside a multi-billion-pound aerospace programme and now inside a 20-person robotics startup, the surprising thing is how much is identical. The failure modes, the human dynamics, the things that actually determine success or failure — they don't change with the number of zeros on the budget. The stuff that's different is mostly cosmetic.

**Angle:** This is a compare-and-contrast essay. You have a genuinely rare vantage point: Rolls-Royce UltraFan (huge programme, massive organisation, world-class resources) vs. AUAR V3 (tiny team, constrained budget, shipping fast). Most people only see one end of the spectrum. You've seen both. The Dwarkesh energy here is: extract the universal truths, and be honest about what surprised you.

**Key beats to hit:**

- Open with a parallel — a moment at Rolls-Royce and a moment at AUAR that felt uncannily similar despite the completely different scale. Set the question: what actually stays the same?
- Constant 1: The critical path is always people, not technology. At Rolls-Royce you had hundreds of engineers; at AUAR you have single digits. In both cases, the constraint that determined whether you shipped on time was whether the right 2–3 people were unblocked and aligned. Scaling the headcount doesn't solve this — it often makes it worse.
- Constant 2: Procurement is always the bottleneck you underestimate. Whether you're buying turbine blades or timber panel components, the lead time and supplier reliability problem is structurally identical. The tools change (SAP vs. Notion), but the failure mode is the same: you don't chase hard enough, early enough.
- Constant 3: The first unit is always a mess, and that's fine. The UltraFan first build was chaos. The V3 Beta will be chaos. The organisations that scale are the ones that build learning loops into the mess rather than pretending it won't happen.
- Constant 4: Culture eats process. At Rolls-Royce, the best-performing cells had strong team cultures, not the best SOPs. At AUAR, same thing. The process is the scaffold, but the culture is the structure.
- What's actually different: resources (obviously), clock speed (startup iterations are faster), risk tolerance (a startup can accept failure modes that would be unthinkable in aerospace). Be honest that these differences matter — but argue they're less determinative than people think.
- Close with the implication: if you've learned to scale manufacturing in one context, the skills transfer more directly than you'd expect. The mental models are portable.

**Tone check:** Confident but not dismissive of scale. You're not saying "big companies are the same as startups" — you're saying the *core manufacturing scaling challenges* are universal, and that's a non-obvious and useful insight.
