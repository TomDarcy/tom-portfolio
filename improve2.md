# tomdarcy.me — Content Improvement Recommendations

*March 2026*

---

> **Executive Summary**
>
> The site works well as an online CV — clean design, strong narrative arc, good credentials. The goal isn't to optimise for active job searching; it's to ensure the site works hard in the background so that when someone Googles you, checks a referral, or lands on your profile via LinkedIn, they get a compelling and complete picture.
>
> Two improvements would materially strengthen that passive positioning: **clearer framing of what makes you distinctive** and **evidence of how you think, not just what you've done.**

---

## Recommendation 1: Sharpen the Positioning

### Situation

The site is your always-on professional presence. You're not actively looking, but the site will be visited by people checking you out: potential collaborators, conference organisers, future hiring managers, investors doing diligence on AUAR's team. For all of these, the site needs to answer two questions fast: "What does this person do?" and "Why should I remember them?"

### Complication

Right now, the hero section answers neither question directly. "Making things that make things" is a strong brand line, but it's a puzzle, not a positioning statement. A visitor has to scroll through the about section and multiple role cards before they piece together what makes you distinctive. The about copy tells a chronological career story ("My career started on the shop floor…") — that's the structure of a CV, not a personal brand. And the CTA section ("Open to: advisory, speaking, collaborations") reads as actively seeking opportunities, which conflicts with the passive posture you actually want.

### Resolution

Three changes, all copy-only:

#### 1. Add a positioning line below the tagline

Keep "Making things that make things" as the brand line. Add a one-sentence descriptor underneath that tells a stranger exactly what you do. This is the line that sticks when someone closes the tab. Examples:

- *"Operations leader and robotics builder. I take manufacturing technology from prototype to production."*
- *"Head of Manufacturing at AUAR. Previously Rolls-Royce, Edwards Vacuum, BCG."*

The second option is more CV-appropriate: it anchors your current role and gives instant credibility through the brand names. It's the version a recruiter or hiring manager processes fastest.

#### 2. Reframe the about copy around your distinctive combination

The current copy buries the most interesting thing about you — the bridge between technical and operational — at the end of the third paragraph. Lead with it. A suggested reframe: open with the "I write Python and I run factories" line (currently hidden at the bottom), then use the career arc as proof. Same content, just reordered so the distinctive bit lands first. When someone skims for 10 seconds, they should walk away with: "Technical operations leader, serious brand-name pedigree, now building robots."

#### 3. Soften the CTA to match a passive posture

Replace "Open to: advisory roles, speaking opportunities, robotics collaborations" with something lower-key that doesn't signal availability. For example:

- *"Always happy to talk manufacturing, robotics, or interesting problems."*

Keep the LinkedIn and email links. Drop the explicit list of what you're open to — it reads as a pitch, which undercuts the "I'm not looking" signal. The right tone is approachable but not available.

### Effort & Impact

| Change | Effort | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Positioning line | 15 mins | High — first impression |
| About copy reorder | 30–45 mins | High — stickiness |
| CTA tone shift | 10 mins | Medium — signal alignment |

## Recommendation 2: Add a "Thinking" Section

### Situation

An online CV tells people what you've done. That's necessary but not sufficient. The strongest personal sites also show how someone thinks. This matters because the people who land on your site — whether they're doing a reference check, considering you for a board, or just curious — are trying to calibrate your judgement, not just your credentials.

### Complication

The site is currently static. Once someone has read the five role cards, there's nothing else to discover. There's no content that demonstrates strategic thinking, technical depth, or a point of view. Your LinkedIn posts partially serve this purpose, but they live on someone else's platform, get buried in algorithmic feeds, and can't be curated by you. For a passive CV, this is a missed opportunity: the site should be the definitive source on you, and right now it only covers half the picture.

### Resolution

Add a "Thinking" or "Writing" section. This doesn't need to be a blog with a publishing cadence. It's a curated collection that rounds out the CV. Three options in order of effort:

#### Option A: Curated links (lowest effort)

A grid of 4–6 cards linking to your best LinkedIn posts, any published articles, or external mentions. Add a one-line summary to each. Takes ~2 hours and immediately gives visitors more to explore. This is the "selected works" approach — minimal maintenance, high signal.

#### Option B: Short-form essays (medium effort)

Write 3–4 pieces (500–800 words) on topics where you have a distinctive angle. These sit on the site permanently and compound in value over time. Candidate topics:

- Why most manufacturing innovation dies between prototype and production
- What running a 24/7 R&D factory at 25 actually looked like
- The case for writing Python on the factory floor
- Robotic construction: what's real and what's hype

Each topic sits at the intersection of your experience and genuine curiosity. They're the kind of pieces that get shared and that a hiring manager or board member would reference in conversation.

#### Option C: Flagship case study (highest effort, highest long-term value)

One in-depth piece (1,500–2,000 words) on the UltraFan assembly programme or the V3 MicroFactory build. Structure as problem → approach → result with enough operational detail to demonstrate real expertise. This becomes the piece you link in bios and that people reference when recommending you. One great case study is worth more than a dozen role descriptions.

### Recommended Approach

Start with Option A now (2 hours, no ongoing commitment). When you have a quiet weekend, write one essay from the Option B list. Target a flagship case study for Q3 2026 when the V3 story has a shipping milestone to anchor it. This gives you a progressively richer "Thinking" section without ever feeling like a content treadmill.

### Effort & Impact

| Option | Effort | Impact | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: Curated links | 2 hours | Medium | This week |
| B: Short essays | 2–3 hrs/piece | High | Ad hoc |
| C: Case study | 1–2 days | Very High | Q3 2026 |

---

*Both recommendations are independent. The highest-leverage single change is the positioning line (Reco 1, Change 1) — 15 minutes of work that improves every visit to the site from here on.*
